### Title
Yield Theft via Sandwich Attack on Public `updateRSETHPrice()` - (File: contracts/LRTOracle.sol)

---

### Summary

`LRTOracle.updateRSETHPrice()` is a permissionless public function. When staking rewards accrue and increase the protocol's TVL above the previously recorded level, any caller can trigger a price update that raises `rsETHPrice`. An unprivileged attacker can atomically deposit at the old (lower) price, call `updateRSETHPrice()` themselves to lock in the new higher price, then immediately initiate a withdrawal at the elevated rate — capturing yield that belongs to existing rsETH holders.

---

### Finding Description

**Root cause — `updateRSETHPrice()` has no access control:** [1](#0-0) 

Any address can call this function at any time. The function recomputes `rsETHPrice` as: [2](#0-1) 

When `totalETHInProtocol > previousTVL` (i.e., rewards have accrued), `rsETHPrice` increases.

**Deposit minting uses the pre-update (lower) price:** [3](#0-2) 

A lower `rsETHPrice` means more rsETH minted per unit of asset — the attacker deposits at the stale low price and receives an inflated rsETH balance.

**Withdrawal amount is locked at the post-update (higher) price:** [4](#0-3) 

`expectedAssetAmount` is computed at initiation time and stored in the withdrawal request: [5](#0-4) 

The payout logic takes `min(expectedAssetAmount, currentReturn)`, so the attacker's gain is locked in at initiation and is only reduced if the price subsequently falls: [6](#0-5) 

**Attack sequence (entirely self-contained, no front-running required):**

1. Attacker observes on-chain that `totalETHInProtocol > rsethSupply × rsETHPrice` (rewards have accrued but price not yet updated).
2. Attacker calls `LRTDepositPool.depositETH()` at the stale low `rsETHPrice` → receives inflated rsETH.
3. Attacker calls `LRTOracle.updateRSETHPrice()` → `rsETHPrice` jumps to reflect accrued rewards.
4. Attacker calls `LRTWithdrawalManager.initiateWithdrawal()` → `expectedAssetAmount` is locked at the new high price.
5. After the ~8-day withdrawal delay, attacker calls `completeWithdrawal()` and receives more assets than deposited.

**Numerical example:**

| State | rsETH Supply | TVL (ETH) | rsETHPrice |
|---|---|---|---|
| Before rewards | 1 000 | 1 000 | 1.000 |
| After rewards accrue | 1 000 | 1 010 | 1.000 (stale) |
| Attacker deposits 1 000 ETH | 2 000 | 2 010 | 1.000 (stale) |
| After `updateRSETHPrice()` | 2 000 | 2 010 | **1.005** |
| Attacker withdraws 1 000 rsETH | — | — | receives **1 005 ETH** |

Attacker profit: **5 ETH**. Existing stakers receive 1 005 ETH instead of 1 010 ETH — **5 ETH of yield stolen**.

---

### Impact Explanation

**High — Theft of unclaimed yield.** Existing rsETH holders lose a portion of every reward cycle proportional to the attacker's deposit size. The attack is repeatable on every reward distribution event. The attacker recovers their principal plus a share of the accrued rewards, while legitimate long-term stakers receive a diluted yield.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

**Medium.** The attacker needs only to:
- Monitor on-chain state for reward accrual (trivially observable via `getTotalAssetDeposits` vs. stored `rsETHPrice × totalSupply`).
- Hold capital at risk for ~8 days (the `withdrawalDelayBlocks` default of `8 days / 12 seconds`). [7](#0-6) 

No privileged access, no oracle manipulation, and no front-running of other users' transactions is required. The attacker controls the entire sequence themselves. The 8-day delay is the only friction; for large deposits and meaningful reward amounts the attack is economically rational.

---

### Recommendation

1. **Restrict `updateRSETHPrice()`** to a permissioned role (e.g., `MANAGER` or a dedicated keeper), matching the already-existing `updateRSETHPriceAsManager()` pattern: [8](#0-7) 

2. **Alternatively**, enforce a minimum deposit holding period before a withdrawal can be initiated, preventing same-block or same-transaction deposit-then-withdraw sequences.

3. **Consider** snapshotting `rsETHPrice` at deposit time and using `min(depositPrice, currentPrice)` when computing withdrawal amounts, so depositors cannot benefit from price increases they did not earn.

---

### Proof of Concept

```
// Attacker EOA, single transaction bundle (e.g., via a smart contract):

// Step 1: Deposit at stale low rsETHPrice
lrtDepositPool.depositETH{value: 1000 ether}(0, "attack");
// Receives 1000 rsETH (at price 1.000)

// Step 2: Trigger price update — public, no access control
lrtOracle.updateRSETHPrice();
// rsETHPrice now = 1.005 (rewards reflected)

// Step 3: Lock in withdrawal at new high price
rsETH.approve(address(lrtWithdrawalManager), 1000e18);
lrtWithdrawalManager.initiateWithdrawal(ETH_TOKEN, 1000e18, "attack");
// expectedAssetAmount = 1000 * 1.005 / 1.0 = 1005 ETH — locked in storage

// Step 4: After ~8 days
lrtWithdrawalManager.completeWithdrawal(ETH_TOKEN, "attack");
// Receives 1005 ETH — 5 ETH profit at the expense of existing stakers
```
