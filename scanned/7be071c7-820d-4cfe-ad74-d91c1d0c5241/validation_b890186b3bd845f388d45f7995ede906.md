### Title
`unlockQueue` Reverts When Contract Is Paused, Temporarily Freezing User rsETH - (File: contracts/LRTWithdrawalManager.sol)

---

### Summary
The `unlockQueue` function in `LRTWithdrawalManager.sol` carries a `whenNotPaused` modifier. When the contract is paused (a normal emergency response), the withdrawal queue cannot be processed. Users who have already called `initiateWithdrawal` — transferring their rsETH into the contract — have no path to recover those tokens: the queue cannot be unlocked, `completeWithdrawal` is also gated by `whenNotPaused`, and no cancellation function exists.

---

### Finding Description

The withdrawal lifecycle in `LRTWithdrawalManager` is a two-step process:

**Step 1 — `initiateWithdrawal`:** The user's rsETH is pulled into the contract. [1](#0-0) 

**Step 2a — `unlockQueue` (operator):** Processes the queue, burns rsETH, and redeems assets from the unstaking vault. This function is decorated with `whenNotPaused`: [2](#0-1) 

**Step 2b — `completeWithdrawal` (user):** Transfers the unlocked asset to the user. Also gated by `whenNotPaused`: [3](#0-2) 

When the PAUSER_ROLE legitimately pauses the contract in response to an emergency, both `unlockQueue` and `completeWithdrawal` revert. A grep search for `cancel` or `rescind` in `LRTWithdrawalManager.sol` returns no results — there is no function that lets a user reclaim their rsETH from a pending request. The rsETH deposited at `initiateWithdrawal` is stranded in the contract for the entire duration of the pause.

This is the direct analog of the reported "deleverage reverts in closed hours" pattern: a critical queue-processing mechanism (`unlockQueue` ↔ deleverage) is blocked by a state guard (`whenNotPaused` ↔ market-closed check) that was never intended to apply to it, leaving user funds temporarily frozen.

---

### Impact Explanation
**Medium — Temporary freezing of funds.**

Every user who has an in-flight withdrawal request at the moment of a pause has their rsETH locked in `LRTWithdrawalManager` with no recourse until the contract is unpaused. The freeze duration is bounded only by how long the pause lasts; there is no self-help path for the user.

---

### Likelihood Explanation
Pausing is a routine emergency response available to any address holding `PAUSER_ROLE`. The protocol explicitly provisions this role for security incidents. Any such incident that triggers a pause while withdrawal requests are pending — a realistic and foreseeable combination — activates the freeze. No attacker action is required; the pauser acts in good faith.

---

### Recommendation
Remove `whenNotPaused` from `unlockQueue` so that the queue can be drained even during a pause, allowing users to subsequently call `completeWithdrawal` once the contract is unpaused (or remove `whenNotPaused` from `completeWithdrawal` as well for already-unlocked requests). Alternatively, add a `cancelWithdrawal` function that returns the user's rsETH when the contract is paused, mirroring the fix applied to the analogous deleverage bug.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Alice calls `initiateWithdrawal(stETH, 1e18, "ref")`.
   - `LRTWithdrawalManager` pulls `1e18` rsETH from Alice via `safeTransferFrom`.
   - A `WithdrawalRequest` is recorded; Alice's rsETH is now held by the contract.

2. A security incident occurs; PAUSER_ROLE calls `pause()`.

3. Operator attempts `unlockQueue(stETH, ...)` → **reverts** (`Pausable: paused`) at the `whenNotPaused` check. [4](#0-3) 

4. Alice attempts `completeWithdrawal(stETH, "ref")` → **reverts** (`Pausable: paused`). [5](#0-4) 

5. No `cancelWithdrawal` function exists. Alice's `1e18` rsETH remains locked in `LRTWithdrawalManager` for the entire pause duration with no recovery path.
