### Title
Operator-Gated `unlockQueue` Creates Permanent Fund Freeze for Withdrawal Requestors - (File: contracts/LRTWithdrawalManager.sol)

### Summary
Users who call `initiateWithdrawal` irrevocably transfer their rsETH into `LRTWithdrawalManager`. Completing that withdrawal requires the operator or asset-transfer role to first call `unlockQueue`. If those privileged roles withhold that call indefinitely, and instant withdrawal is not enabled, the deposited rsETH is permanently frozen with no user-accessible escape path.

### Finding Description
The withdrawal lifecycle in `LRTWithdrawalManager` is a two-phase process:

**Phase 1 – User-initiated (permissionless):**
`initiateWithdrawal` pulls rsETH from the user into the contract and records a request keyed by `nextUnusedNonce[asset]`. [1](#0-0) 

**Phase 2 – Operator-gated (privileged):**
`unlockQueue` advances `nextLockedNonce[asset]`, which is the only mechanism that marks requests as claimable. [2](#0-1) 

**Phase 3 – User completion (blocked until Phase 2):**
`_processWithdrawalCompletion` enforces that the user's nonce is strictly less than `nextLockedNonce[asset]`; otherwise it reverts with `WithdrawalLocked`. [3](#0-2) 

There is no cancellation function. The only alternative exit is `instantWithdrawal`, but it is gated by `onlyInstantWithdrawalAllowed(asset)`, a manager-controlled toggle that is off by default. [4](#0-3) 

The roles that can call `unlockQueue` are `ASSET_TRANSFER_ROLE` and `OPERATOR_ROLE`, both assigned and revocable by the protocol admin. [5](#0-4) 

### Impact Explanation
Any user who has called `initiateWithdrawal` has already surrendered their rsETH to the contract. If `unlockQueue` is never called for their asset, their rsETH remains locked in the contract indefinitely with no on-chain recourse. This constitutes **permanent freezing of funds** (Critical) or at minimum **temporary freezing of funds** (Medium) for every affected withdrawal requestor.

### Likelihood Explanation
The operator role is a hot-wallet key used in normal protocol operations. A key compromise, regulatory action against the operator, or a deliberate protocol decision to halt withdrawals for a specific asset would all trigger this freeze without any user-side action being able to prevent it. The freeze affects all queued requests for the affected asset simultaneously.

### Recommendation
Introduce a user-cancellation path: allow a withdrawal requestor to reclaim their rsETH if their request has not been unlocked within a defined timeout (e.g., `withdrawalDelayBlocks` after submission). This mirrors the Infinex patch approach of removing the hard dependency on a privileged signature for fund recovery.

### Proof of Concept
1. Alice calls `initiateWithdrawal(ETH_TOKEN, 1e18, "")`. Her rsETH is transferred to the contract; her nonce is `N`. `nextLockedNonce[ETH_TOKEN]` remains at some value `≤ N`.
2. The operator stops calling `unlockQueue` (key lost, regulatory freeze, or deliberate action). `nextLockedNonce[ETH_TOKEN]` never advances past `N`.
3. Alice calls `completeWithdrawal(ETH_TOKEN, "")`. `_processWithdrawalCompletion` executes line 707: `N >= nextLockedNonce[ETH_TOKEN]` → `revert WithdrawalLocked()`.
4. `instantWithdrawal` is not enabled (`isInstantWithdrawalEnabled[ETH_TOKEN] == false`), so that path also reverts.
5. Alice has no further on-chain options. Her rsETH is permanently locked in `LRTWithdrawalManager`. [6](#0-5) [7](#0-6)
