### Title
Permissionless `FeeReceiver.sendFunds()` + `LRTOracle.updateRSETHPrice()` Enables MEV Reward Front-Running to Steal Yield from rsETH Holders — (`contracts/FeeReceiver.sol`, `contracts/LRTOracle.sol`)

---

### Summary

An attacker can monitor the `FeeReceiver` contract for accumulated MEV/EL rewards, deposit ETH at the current (pre-reward) rsETH price, then atomically trigger `FeeReceiver.sendFunds()` and `LRTOracle.updateRSETHPrice()` to capture the reward in the rsETH price. Because the deposit was priced before the reward was reflected in TVL, the attacker receives more rsETH per ETH than they should, diluting the yield that should have accrued proportionally to all existing rsETH holders.

---

### Finding Description

**Step 1 — `FeeReceiver.sendFunds()` is fully permissionless.** [1](#0-0) 

No role check, no pause gate. Any EOA or contract can call it at any time, transferring the entire `FeeReceiver` ETH balance into `LRTDepositPool` via `receiveFromRewardReceiver()`.

**Step 2 — `LRTOracle.updateRSETHPrice()` is fully permissionless.** [2](#0-1) 

Only guarded by `whenNotPaused`. Any caller can trigger a price update.

**Step 3 — Deposit pricing uses the stale stored `rsETHPrice`.** [3](#0-2) 

`getRsETHAmountToMint` divides by `lrtOracle.rsETHPrice()` — the last *stored* price, not a freshly computed one. A deposit made before `sendFunds()` is priced at the pre-reward rate.

**Step 4 — `_updateRsETHPrice()` computes new price from live TVL.** [4](#0-3) 

After `sendFunds()` moves ETH into the deposit pool, `_getTotalEthInProtocol()` returns a higher value, and `newRsETHPrice` rises. The attacker's rsETH — minted at the old price — is now worth more ETH per token.

**Attack sequence (no same-block exit required):**

1. Observe `FeeReceiver.balance` accumulating a meaningful MEV reward.
2. Call `LRTDepositPool.depositETH()` — rsETH minted at current stale price.
3. Call `FeeReceiver.sendFunds()` — reward ETH enters deposit pool, TVL rises.
4. Call `LRTOracle.updateRSETHPrice()` — stored price updates to reflect new TVL.
5. Wait out `withdrawalDelayBlocks` (default ~8 days).
6. Claim withdrawal at the higher rsETH price — profit equals the attacker's pro-rata share of the injected reward, which was not earned by the attacker.

The attacker does not need same-block atomicity. Steps 2–4 can be in one transaction; step 6 follows after the delay. Existing holders who held through the reward period receive a diluted share of the yield.

---

### Impact Explanation

Every rsETH holder who held through the MEV reward accrual period loses a fraction of their yield to the attacker. The attacker's deposit, priced before the reward, entitles them to a share of the reward they did not earn. This is a direct, quantifiable theft of unclaimed yield proportional to the attacker's deposit size relative to total rsETH supply at the time of the price update.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

**High.** Both `sendFunds()` and `updateRSETHPrice()` are permissionless. MEV/EL rewards accumulate continuously in `FeeReceiver`. The attack requires no privileged access, no oracle manipulation, and no reentrancy — only monitoring the `FeeReceiver` balance and submitting two transactions. The only partial mitigation is `pricePercentageLimit`: [5](#0-4) 

If the reward would push the price above `pricePercentageLimit * highestRsethPrice`, `updateRSETHPrice()` reverts for non-managers. However:
- If `pricePercentageLimit == 0` (unset), there is no limit at all.
- For rewards below the threshold, the attack succeeds unconditionally.
- An attacker can repeat the attack many times with smaller rewards to accumulate profit.

The withdrawal delay prevents same-block profit realization but does not prevent the attack.

---

### Recommendation

1. **Restrict `FeeReceiver.sendFunds()`** to a privileged role (e.g., `MANAGER` or `OPERATOR`) so that reward injection timing is controlled by the protocol, not by arbitrary callers.
2. **Alternatively**, call `updateRSETHPrice()` atomically inside `receiveFromRewardReceiver()` or `sendFunds()` so that any deposit made before the reward injection is priced *after* the reward is reflected — eliminating the timing gap.
3. **Ensure `pricePercentageLimit` is always set** to a non-zero value as a defense-in-depth measure.

### Proof of Concept

```solidity
// SPDX-License-Identifier: MIT
pragma solidity 0.8.27;

// Pseudocode for a fork test (Foundry)
function testMEVFrontRun() public {
    // Setup: FeeReceiver has 10 ETH of accumulated MEV rewards
    vm.deal(address(feeReceiver), 10 ether);

    uint256 priceBefore = lrtOracle.rsETHPrice();
    uint256 rsethSupplyBefore = rsETH.totalSupply();

    // Step 1: Attacker deposits 100 ETH at stale price
    vm.prank(attacker);
    depositPool.depositETH{value: 100 ether}(0, "");
    uint256 attackerRsETH = rsETH.balanceOf(attacker);

    // Step 2: Attacker triggers reward injection
    vm.prank(attacker);
    feeReceiver.sendFunds();

    // Step 3: Attacker triggers price update
    vm.prank(attacker);
    lrtOracle.updateRSETHPrice();

    uint256 priceAfter = lrtOracle.rsETHPrice();
    assert(priceAfter > priceBefore); // price rose due to MEV reward

    // Step 4: After withdrawal delay, attacker redeems
    // attackerRsETH * priceAfter > 100 ether => profit > 0
    uint256 attackerETHValue = attackerRsETH * priceAfter / 1e18;
    assert(attackerETHValue > 100 ether); // attacker captured yield they didn't earn
}
```

The assertion `attackerETHValue > 100 ether` will hold for any non-zero `FeeReceiver` balance at the time of the attack, as long as the price increase stays within `pricePercentageLimit`.
