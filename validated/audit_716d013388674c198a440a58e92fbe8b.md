## Analysis

Let me trace the full data flow carefully.

**Step 1: Who generates `seed._ij` and who uses it?**

In `ecdsa_mp.cpp`, the OT-receiver generates the seed locally and sends it to the OT-sender: [1](#0-0) 

The OT-receiver calls `crypto::gen_random(seed._ij)` (line 249), uses it to generate `v[l][t]` and `sigma`, then sends the seed to the OT-sender via `plain_broadcast_and_pairwise_message`. The OT-sender then reconstructs the DRBG from `seed._j` (line 294).

**Step 2: The 32-byte fast path is real**

`drbg_aes_ctr_t::init(mem_t s)` does skip hashing for 32-byte inputs: [2](#0-1) 

Since `seed` is `buf256_t` (32 bytes), the fast path `ctr.init(s.take(16), s.data+16)` is taken — no SHA-256 mixing.

**Step 3: The premise of the attack is self-defeating**

The question's attack model is: "malicious OT-receiver controls `seed._ij` → predicts sender's DRBG outputs (`v[l][t]`, `sigma`) → recovers `x_i`."

But the receiver **already knows** every value the sender's DRBG produces, because the receiver generated them first:

- The receiver generates `v[l][t]` and `sigma` from `seed._ij` (lines 256, 269)
- The receiver sends `seed._ij` to the sender precisely so the sender can reproduce the same `v[l][t]` and `sigma`
- Controlling the seed gives the receiver zero additional information — they already possess all DRBG outputs

**Step 4: Even with full knowledge of `v[l][t]` and `sigma`, `x_i` is not recoverable**

The sender computes: [3](#0-2) 

`s[ot_sender][j][t] = -Σ(v[l][t] · delta[l][j][t]) - sigma`

The `delta[l][j][t]` values are computed locally by the sender as `X0[l*4+t] + a_sender[t]`: [4](#0-3) 

These `delta` values are **never sent to the receiver**. The receiver only receives `ot_msg3` (the OT extension wire message), which lets them compute their OT output `X[l][j][t]` — either `X0` or `X0 + 2·a_sender[t]` — but not `X0` and `X0 + 2·a_sender[t]` simultaneously. Without `delta[l][j][t]`, the receiver cannot compute `s[ot_sender][j][t]`, and therefore cannot form the sum `s[ot_receiver] + s[ot_sender]` that would reveal `a_sender[t]`.

**Step 5: The blinding structure holds**

Even if the receiver could somehow compute `s[ot_sender][j][t]`, the sender's contribution to `rho_x_i` is: [5](#0-4) 

`rho_x_i = rho_i · x_i + Σ(s[role][j][2] + s[role][j][3])`

The independent random blinding scalar `rho_i` (line 171) prevents recovering `x_i` from `rho_x_i` alone, even if all `s` terms were known.
