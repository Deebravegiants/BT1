Looking at the research, I found a concrete analog in nearcore's meta-transaction (delegate action) system.

### Title
Deposit Refund Misdirection in Meta-Transactions: Relayer Loses Attached Balance to Delegate Sender on Inner Receipt Failure - (File: runtime/runtime/src/actions.rs, runtime/runtime/src/lib.rs)

### Summary

When a meta-transaction (NEP-366 `DelegateAction`) includes an attached deposit and the inner action receipt fails on the receiver's shard, the deposit refund is sent to the delegate sender (`Alice`), not to the relayer who actually paid the deposit. An unprivileged user can exploit this by crafting a `DelegateAction` with a large attached balance that is designed to fail on the destination shard, draining the relayer's NEAR balance.

### Finding Description

In NEAR's meta-transaction flow, the relayer wraps a user's `SignedDelegateAction` in a transaction and pays all costs including any attached deposit. When `apply_delegate_action` processes the delegate action on Alice's shard, it creates a new inner action receipt with `predecessor_id = sender_id` (Alice): [1](#0-0) 

This inner receipt is then forwarded to Bob's shard. If the inner receipt fails (e.g., the called method does not exist, the contract panics, or any state-dependent check fails), `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` generates a deposit refund receipt: [2](#0-1) 

The refund destination is determined by `balance_refund_receiver()`: [3](#0-2) 

Since the inner receipt's `predecessor_id` is Alice (not the relayer), the full deposit refund is sent to Alice. The relayer, who paid the deposit, receives nothing back.

The nearcore documentation explicitly acknowledges this as a known protocol-level issue: [4](#0-3) 

The deposit refund logic in `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` computes `deposit_refund = total_deposit` when the result is an error: [5](#0-4) 

There is no mechanism to redirect the deposit refund to the relayer (the actual payer) when the inner receipt of a meta-transaction fails.

### Impact Explanation

**Impact: High.** An unprivileged user (Alice) can steal arbitrary amounts of NEAR from a relayer by:

1. Crafting a `DelegateAction` with a large attached deposit (e.g., 100 NEAR) targeting a function call that Alice knows will fail on the receiver's shard (e.g., calling a non-existent method, or a method whose execution depends on state that Alice can manipulate to cause a revert).
2. Submitting the `SignedDelegateAction` to the relayer.
3. The relayer pays the 100 NEAR deposit when submitting the transaction.
4. The inner receipt fails on Bob's shard.
5. The 100 NEAR deposit refund is sent to Alice, not the relayer.

The corrupted protocol value is the **balance** of both the relayer (loses the deposit) and Alice (gains the deposit). This is a direct, permanent, irreversible transfer of funds from the relayer to Alice.

### Likelihood Explanation

**Likelihood: Medium.** The attack requires:
- A relayer that accepts meta-transactions with attached deposits (common for FT transfer use cases).
- Alice to craft an inner action that will fail on the destination shard. This is straightforward: calling a non-existent method, calling a method on a contract that will revert based on state Alice controls, or targeting a receiver that has no contract deployed.

The attack is repeatable and scales with the deposit amount the relayer is willing to accept. The nearcore documentation itself warns relayer implementations about this exact financial incentive, confirming the attack is realistic.

### Recommendation

1. When processing a `DelegateAction`, record the relayer's account ID in the inner receipt (e.g., via the `refund_to` field on `ActionReceiptV2`) so that deposit refunds on failure are directed to the relayer rather than the delegate sender.
2. Alternatively, enforce that inner receipts spawned from delegate actions carry a `refund_to` override pointing to the relayer (`receipt.predecessor_id()` of the outer delegate receipt), so the runtime's existing `balance_refund_receiver()` logic returns the correct party.
3. At minimum, relayer implementations should be warned (and the protocol documentation updated) that accepting meta-transactions with attached deposits is financially dangerous without additional safeguards.

### Proof of Concept

1. Alice creates a `DelegateAction` targeting `bob.near` with a `FunctionCall` action attaching 100 NEAR to a method `"nonexistent_method"`.
2. Alice signs the `DelegateAction` and sends it to the relayer.
3. The relayer wraps it in a transaction (relayer is the signer, Alice's account is the receiver of the outer receipt).
4. On Alice's shard, `apply_delegate_action` creates an inner receipt: `predecessor_id = alice.near`, `receiver_id = bob.near`, deposit = 100 NEAR. The relayer's balance is debited 100 NEAR.
5. On Bob's shard, the inner receipt fails (`AccountDoesNotExist` or `MethodNotFound`).
6. `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` fires: `deposit_refund = 100 NEAR`, sent to `receipt.balance_refund_receiver()` = `alice.near`.
7. Alice's balance increases by 100 NEAR. The relayer's balance is permanently reduced by 100 NEAR.

This is confirmed by the test `test_gas_key_refund` which shows deposit refunds go to the sender account on failure: [6](#0-5) 

And by the delegate action test confirming `predecessor_id = sender_id` on the inner receipt: [7](#0-6)
