### Summary

### Impact Explanation

The velocity guard is the sole on-chain circuit breaker against rapid oracle price moves (e.g., a compromised Pyth publisher or a flash-loan-assisted oracle manipulation). When the guard is too permissive, a manipulated oracle mid-price that exceeds the intended per-second velocity limit passes the `beforeSwap` hook unchallenged and reaches `_executeSwap`. The pool then executes at the manipulated bid/ask, causing LP funds to be drained at an off-market price â€” a direct loss of LP principal. The magnitude of the excess permitted move (1.87â€“2.55Ã— on Base/HyperEVM) is large enough to matter for any realistic `maxChangePerBlockE18` setting.

Severity: **Medium** â€” requires oracle price manipulation as a precondition, but the velocity guard exists precisely to be the last line of defense against that scenario; its systematic failure on the protocol's own L2 deployment targets removes that defense entirely.

### Recommendation

Replace `block.number` with `block.timestamp` throughout `PriceVelocityGuardExtension`. Store `lastUpdateTimestamp` (seconds) instead of `lastUpdateBlock`, and express `maxChangePerBlockE18` as `maxChangePerSecondE18`. The velocity formula becomes:

```solidity
uint256 timeDiff = block.timestamp - prevTimestamp;
uint256 allowedSq = uint256(maxChange) * uint256(maxChange) * (1 + timeDiff);
if (actualSq > allowedSq) revert PriceVelocityExceeded(...);
```

This is chain-agnostic: `block.timestamp` is reliable on all EVM-compatible L2s (Base, Optimism, HyperEVM) and directly represents real elapsed seconds, making the random-walk budget model correct regardless of block production rate.
