### Title
Proposal Deposits Permanently Lost When `do_update` Sweeps All Pending Proposals — (`crates/contract/src/update.rs`)

### Summary

When `vote_update` reaches threshold and `do_update` executes, it unconditionally clears **all** pending proposal entries (`self.entries.clear()`). Because `UpdateEntry` does not record the proposer's account ID or the deposit amount, the NEAR deposits paid by every non-winning proposer are permanently locked in the contract with no refund path.

### Finding Description

`propose_update` requires a deposit proportional to the storage cost of the proposal (up to ~17 NEAR for a full contract binary): [1](#0-0) 

The deposit is kept in the contract. The `UpdateEntry` stored in `proposed_updates.entries` records only the update payload and `bytes_used`, **not** the proposer's identity or the deposit amount: [2](#0-1) 

When any update reaches threshold, `do_update` removes the winning entry and then calls `self.entries.clear()` and `self.vote_by_participant.clear()`, discarding every other pending proposal: [3](#0-2) 

Because the proposer identity is never stored, there is no mechanism to refund the deposits of the cleared proposals. The NEAR tokens are permanently locked in the contract account.

### Impact Explanation

Every MPC participant who proposed a competing update loses their full deposit — up to `CURRENT_CONTRACT_DEPLOY_DEPOSIT` (~17 NEAR) per proposal — the moment any other proposal is executed. The funds are not recoverable without a subsequent contract upgrade that explicitly redistributes them. This breaks the production accounting invariant that deposited funds are either consumed for their stated purpose or returned to the depositor. [4](#0-3) 

### Likelihood Explanation

In normal MPC governance, multiple participants independently propose updates (e.g., different contract versions or config changes). The test suite itself exercises this pattern with `PROPOSAL_COUNT = 2` concurrent proposals. Whenever the threshold is reached for one proposal, all others are silently cleared and their deposits are lost. No attacker capability is required — this is triggered by the ordinary governance flow. [5](#0-4) 

### Recommendation

Store the proposer's `AccountId` and the exact deposit amount inside `UpdateEntry`. In `do_update`, before calling `self.entries.clear()`, iterate over all remaining entries and schedule `Promise::new(proposer).transfer(deposit)` for each one. This mirrors the pattern already used in `propose_update` for refunding excess deposits.

### Proof of Concept

1. Participant A calls `propose_update` with a contract binary, attaching ~17 NEAR. `UpdateEntry { update, bytes_used }` is stored; the proposer's identity is discarded.
2. Participant B calls `propose_update` with a different binary, also attaching ~17 NEAR.
3. Threshold participants vote for B's proposal. `vote_update` calls `do_update(&B_id, gas)`.
4. `do_update` removes B's entry, then calls `self.entries.clear()` — A's entry (and its associated 17 NEAR deposit) is erased with no refund.
5. A's 17 NEAR is permanently locked in the contract. A has no function to call to recover it. [6](#0-5)
