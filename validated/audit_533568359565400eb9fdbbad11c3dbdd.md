### Title
Deposit Refund Misdirected to Sender (Alice) Instead of Relayer (Payer) in Meta Transactions - (`runtime/runtime/src/lib.rs`, `runtime/runtime/src/actions.rs`)

---

### Summary

In NEAR meta transactions (NEP-366), when a relayer submits a `DelegateAction` with an attached deposit on behalf of a user (Alice), and the inner action fails on the receiver's shard, the deposit refund is sent to Alice (the `predecessor_id` of the inner receipt) rather than to the relayer who actually paid for it. This is the direct analog of the MysteryBox bug: a payer's funds are routed to the wrong party instead of being returned to the payer.

---

### Finding Description

When a meta transaction is processed, `apply_delegate_action` creates a new inner receipt with `predecessor_id = sender_id` (Alice): [1](#0-0) 

The inner receipt's `predecessor_id` is Alice, while `signer_id` is the relayer. When this inner receipt fails (e.g., the receiver contract does not exist, or the function call panics), `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` issues the deposit refund to `balance_refund_receiver()`: [2](#0-1) 

`balance_refund_receiver()` returns `predecessor_id()` when no explicit `refund_to` is set: [3](#0-2) 

So the deposit refund receipt is addressed to Alice, not the relayer. The relayer paid the deposit (it was deducted from the relayer's balance when the transaction was processed), but Alice receives it back on failure.

This behavior is explicitly acknowledged in the protocol documentation: [4](#0-3) 

The root cause is that the inner receipt produced by `apply_delegate_action` uses `sender_id` (Alice) as `predecessor_id`, which then becomes the deposit refund target — even though the relayer, not Alice, funded the deposit.

---

### Impact Explanation

The corrupted protocol value is the **balance** of both the relayer and Alice. The relayer's balance is permanently reduced by the deposit amount, and Alice's balance is permanently increased by the same amount, with no legitimate basis. This is a direct token theft from the relayer to Alice.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

Any unprivileged user (Alice) who can submit a `SignedDelegateAction` to a relayer can trigger this. Alice crafts a meta transaction with a large `deposit` attached to an inner `FunctionCall` targeting a receiver that will fail (non-existent account, panicking contract, etc.). The relayer, trusting the action, submits it. The inner receipt fails on the receiver's shard, and the deposit is refunded to Alice. The relayer has no on-chain recourse. The financial incentive for Alice is explicit and proportional to the deposit size.

---

### Recommendation

When `apply_delegate_action` creates the inner receipt, it should set `refund_to` (via `ActionReceiptV2`) to the relayer's account (`action_receipt.signer_id()`), so that deposit refunds on failure are returned to the party who paid, not to Alice. Alternatively, the inner receipt's `predecessor_id` could be set to the relayer for deposit-refund purposes while preserving Alice as the logical sender for contract-level `predecessor_account_id()` checks — though this requires careful protocol design.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Relayer submits a meta transaction: Alice signs a `DelegateAction` with `FunctionCall { deposit: 10 NEAR, method: "nonexistent", receiver: "no-contract.near" }`.
2. The relayer's balance is debited 10 NEAR when the transaction is processed.
3. The inner receipt is created with `predecessor_id = alice.near`, `signer_id = relayer.near`.
4. The inner receipt fails (`AccountDoesNotExist` or function call panic).
5. `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` calls `Receipt::new_balance_refund(receipt.balance_refund_receiver(), 10 NEAR)`.
6. `balance_refund_receiver()` returns `alice.near` (the `predecessor_id`).
7. Alice receives 10 NEAR; the relayer's 10 NEAR is gone.

This is confirmed by the integration test `meta_tx_delete_account` and the explicit documentation warning at `docs/architecture/how/meta-tx.md` lines 232–242. [5](#0-4) [6](#0-5)
